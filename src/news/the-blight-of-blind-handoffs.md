<h3 class="pb-8 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-white ztablet:text-xlg">
The Blight of 'Blind Handoffs'
</h3>
</div>
<div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
A delivery can travel across cities, borders, warehouses, sortation centres, trucks, routing systems, and tracking platforms with impressive precision.
Yet the moment that often determines whether the experience succeeds or fails is far simpler: where the order is left, who can access it, whether it
is protected, and whether the customer can trust that it arrived safely.
<br /><br />
This final step is where much of the promise of modern delivery breaks down. A package may be tracked every kilometre of its journey, only to be
left exposed on a porch. A grocery order may arrive on time, only to sit outside in heat, cold, rain, snow, or uncertainty. A customer may receive
a notification, but still have no true confidence that the handoff was secure.
<br /><br />
This is the blight of blind handoffs.
</div>
<!-- THE PROBLEM -->
<h4 class="pt-6 pb-4 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-brightgreen ztablet:text-lg">
The Problem With Blind Handoffs
</h4>
<div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
In a recent article on digitizing mid- and last-mile logistics handovers,
<a href="https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/logistics/our-insights/digitizing-mid-and-last-mile-logistics-handovers-to-reduce-waste#/" class="underline">McKinsey & Company</a>
described blind handoffs as the moments when two or more parties are involved in transferring goods, and where miscommunication, information loss,
delays, and accountability issues are most likely to occur. McKinsey estimates that these handoff points create between $65 billion and $95 billion
in annual waste in the United States, with B2C delivery waste alone estimated at $18 billion to $28 billion per year.
<br /><br />
That waste is not just an internal logistics problem. It shows up at the customer's front door.
<br /><br />
For consumers, the problem is familiar: missed drop-offs, vague delivery windows, package theft, weather damage, spoiled groceries, and the constant
need to check tracking pages. For sellers, grocers, and retailers, the costs appear as refunds, replacements, customer support tickets, failed
deliveries, poor reviews, and weaker trust. For delivery agents, the issue appears as dwell time, uncertainty, inefficient routes, and unnecessary
friction at the exact moment the delivery should be complete.
<br /><br />
The modern delivery experience has become highly visible, but not always truly dependable. A photo of a package on a doorstep may prove that a driver
was there, but it does not prove that the delivery was secure, protected, or successfully received.
</div>
<!-- SCALE -->
<h4 class="pt-6 pb-4 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-brightgreen ztablet:text-lg">
A Growing Delivery System With a Fragile Final Step
</h4>
<div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
The scale of the challenge is increasing. The
<a href="https://www.pitneybowes.com/us/shipping-index.html" class="underline">Pitney Bowes Parcel Shipping Index</a>
reported that U.S. parcel volume reached 23.1 billion shipments in 2025, up 3.3 percent year over year, with projections showing continued growth
toward 31 billion shipments by 2031. As more goods move directly to homes, the doorstep is becoming a larger and more important part of the logistics
network.
<br /><br />
In Canada, the complexity is different but just as real.
<a href="https://www.canadapost-postescanada.ca/blogs/business/ecommerce/ecommerce-insights/who-really-delivers-the-last-mile-for-your-business/" class="underline">Canada Post</a>
has described the last mile as the place where the delivery experience is decided, noting that Canadian deliveries must work across dense urban
neighbourhoods, apartment buildings, suburban corridors, rural roads, northern communities, and severe weather. The harder the route, the more
important the final handoff becomes.
<br /><br />
The customer experience is also changing. A
<a href="https://newsroom.fedex.com/newsroom/canada-english/fedex-survey-two-thirds-of-canadian-online-holiday-shoppers-expect-to-spend-the-same-or-more-in-2024" class="underline">FedEx Canada survey</a>
found that 81 percent of Canadian respondents expected online retailers to offer tracking features, while 67 percent remained concerned about porch
pirates. The same survey found that 25 percent of consumers checked shipment status multiple times a day, highlighting a simple truth: customers are
not just waiting for deliveries, they are managing delivery anxiety.
<br /><br />
In the United States, the issue is significant enough to attract national attention. The
<a href="https://www.uspsoig.gov/reports/white-papers/package-theft-united-states" class="underline">U.S. Postal Service Office of Inspector General</a>
reported that at least 58 million packages were stolen in 2024, creating financial burdens and operational disruptions across the delivery and
e-commerce ecosystem. The report also noted that package theft is difficult to solve because it sits at the intersection of retailers, delivery
providers, and consumers.
</div>

<h4 class="pt-6 pb-4 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-brightgreen ztablet:text-lg">
Why Grocery Delivery Raises the Stakes
</h4>
<div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
For ordinary parcels, a failed handoff can mean theft, inconvenience, or replacement cost. For groceries and perishables, the consequences can be
more immediate. Fresh food depends on timing, temperature, and trust. A grocery delivery left outside for too long is not simply inconvenient; it can
become waste.
<br /><br />
Online grocery is continuing to grow. Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada reported that U.S. grocery e-commerce grew at a 26.4 percent annual rate
between 2019 and 2023, reaching US$206.8 billion and nearly doubling its share of total grocery sales to 11.8 percent.
<a href="https://agriculture.canada.ca/en/international-trade/market-intelligence/reports-and-guides/distribution-channel-series-grocery-ecommerce-market-united-states" class="underline">The same report</a>
shows that categories such as dairy, prepared meals, soft drinks, and processed meat are already performing especially well online.
<br /><br />
This growth creates a new requirement for the last mile. Grocery delivery cannot rely only on speed. It needs a secure, insulated, accessible, and
verifiable destination at the customer's home. Without that, grocers and delivery providers remain trapped by narrow windows, missed connections, and
the assumption that someone must be home at exactly the right time.
</div>

<!-- OPPORTUNITY -->
<h4 class="pt-6 pb-4 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-brightgreen ztablet:text-lg">
The Opportunity Hidden in the Handoff
</h4>
<div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
Blind handoffs are costly because too much is left to chance. But that also means the opportunity is enormous.
<br /><br />
If the final handoff becomes secure, connected, and dependable, retailers and delivery providers can rethink how deliveries are scheduled and
completed. Instead of routing around narrow customer availability windows, deliveries can happen when they are most efficient. Instead of relying on
doorbells, phone calls, exposed porches, or rushed driver judgement, access can be granted digitally. Instead of ending with uncertainty, a delivery
can end with a verified, protected, and recorded handoff.
<br /><br />
Deloitte has noted that last-mile delivery can account for as much as 30 to 35 percent of total delivery cost, making improvements in reliability,
waste reduction, and customer experience essential to the transportation sector.
<a href="https://www.deloitte.com/na/en/Industries/consumer/perspectives/last-mile-delivery-landscape-transportation-sector.html" class="underline">Its analysis</a>
points to real-time visibility, better transportation intelligence, co-mingled routes, automated parcel lockers, and low-emission delivery models as
part of a more efficient last-mile ecosystem.
<br /><br />
McKinsey reaches a similar conclusion from the perspective of handoff waste. The firm argues that technologies such as real-time visibility
platforms, workflow automation, and contextual communication tools can reduce waste caused by blind handoffs by up to 40 percent when implemented
properly.
<br /><br />
The next step is bringing that intelligence all the way to the customer's doorstep.

From Delivery Tracking to Delivery Trust
</h4>
<div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
The next era of last-mile logistics will not be defined only by better tracking pages, delivery photos, or faster notifications. Those tools are
useful, but they do not fully solve the final handoff.
<br /><br />
The more important shift is from visibility to trust.
<br /><br />
Customers should not have to wonder whether a package is still on the porch. Delivery providers should not have to depend on perfect timing.
Retailers should not have to absorb the cost of failed or uncertain delivery experiences. Grocers should not be limited by the assumption that
someone must be home to receive fresh food.
<br /><br />
Blind handoffs are expensive because they leave too much unresolved at the most important point in the journey. Veranda's goal is to remove that
uncertainty by giving deliveries a secure destination, customers a reliable point of control, and sellers and delivery partners a smarter way to
complete the final step.
