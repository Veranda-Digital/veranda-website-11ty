<h3 class="pb-8 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-white ztablet:text-xlg">
The Blight of 'Blind Handoffs'
</h3>
</div>
<div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
Picture the scene: a driver pulls up, snaps a photo of a box on a doorstep, and drives away. The app says "delivered." But the recipient is still at
work. The groceries sit in the sun. A neighbor's package disappears from the same stoop an hour later. Everyone in the chain—the retailer, the
carrier, the customer—operates on faith that the handoff actually happened. We call this a blind handoff, and it is one of the defining failures of
modern delivery.
</div>
<!-- THE PROBLEM -->
<h4 class="pt-6 pb-4 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-brightgreen ztablet:text-lg">The Problem</h4>
<div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
Blind handoffs create a gap between when a delivery is marked complete and when it is actually received. That gap is where porch piracy, spoilage,
and customer frustration live. Carriers are incentivized to move fast. Retailers want proof of delivery. Consumers want certainty. No single party
owns the moment the package changes hands—and so nobody fully protects it.
</div>

<!-- WHO BEARS THE COST -->
<div class="flex h-auto flex-col items-center justify-between pb-6 zdesktop:flex-row zdesktop:gap-[6em] zdesktop:pb-0">
<!-- Text Column -->
<div class="flex flex-[1.1] flex-col items-start justify-start">
<h4 class="pt-6 pb-4 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-brightgreen ztablet:text-lg">
Who Bears the Cost
</h4>
<div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
Households absorb the immediate cost in stolen goods and ruined food. Retailers eat refunds and support tickets. Carriers face dispute volumes and
reputation damage. The environment pays too—every failed or replaced delivery is another trip, another box, another emission. Blind handoffs are not
a minor inconvenience. They are a structural flaw in how we have built the last mile.
</div>
</div>

<!-- A BETTER MODEL -->
<h4 class="pt-6 pb-4 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-brightgreen ztablet:text-lg">A Better Model</h4>
<div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
The alternative is not complicated: give every delivery a secure, confirmed point of receipt. A place where the carrier can deposit goods, the
customer can retrieve them on their schedule, and both parties have a shared record that the handoff happened. Temperature control, access
management, and notification turn a blind handoff into an accountable one.
</div>

<!-- WHY IT MATTERS NOW -->
<h4 class="pt-6 pb-4 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-brightgreen ztablet:text-lg">Why It Matters Now</h4>
<div class="pb-14 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
Delivery volume is not going down. Grocery e-commerce is growing. Consumer expectations for convenience and security are rising in tandem. Fixing
blind handoffs is no longer a nice-to-have—it is a prerequisite for a last-mile system that can scale without breaking trust at every doorstep. At
Veranda, that is the problem we are solving.
</div>
