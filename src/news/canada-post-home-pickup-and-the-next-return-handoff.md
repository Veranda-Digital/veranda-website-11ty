        <div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
            Canada Post's new
            <a href="https://www.canadapost-postescanada.ca/cpc/en/personal/return-purchase/home-pickup.page" class="underline">Home Pickup</a>
            service is a meaningful step toward making returns feel less like an errand. Instead of printing a label, packing an item, finding time to visit a
            post office, and standing in line, customers can prepare a return or outgoing package, book a pickup in the Canada Post app, and have a mail
            carrier collect it from their home.
            <br /><br />
            The service is simple by design. Canada Post says one pickup can cover up to three packages for a small flat fee, each package must already have a
            Canada Post shipping label attached, and eligible customers can schedule the pickup through the app. For households that are already managing more
            online orders, more returns, and more trips between home and work, that convenience matters.
        </div>

        <h4 class="pt-6 pb-4 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-brightgreen ztablet:text-lg">
            What Home Pickup Changes for Returns
        </h4>
        <div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
            Returns are no longer a small edge case in e-commerce. In the
            <a href="https://info.apprissretail.com/hubfs/Resource-Center/2024-041-2024-consumer-returns-report.pdf" class="underline">
            2024 Consumer Returns in the Retail Industry</a>
            report, Appriss Retail and Deloitte estimated that U.S. merchandise returns amounted to <b class="text-salmon">$685 billion</b> in 2024,
            representing <b class="text-salmon">13.21 percent</b> of total retail sales. Online-originated returns accounted for
            <b class="text-salmon">$362 billion</b> of that total, with a reported online return rate of <b class="text-salmon">24.52 percent</b>.
            <br /><br />
            Canada Post's own returns research shows why this matters. On its
            <a href="https://www.canadapost-postescanada.ca/cpc/en/commercial/simplify-returns.page" class="underline">e-commerce returns solutions</a>
            page, Canada Post says <b class="text-salmon">70 percent</b> of shoppers buy more from brands that make returns easy,
            <b class="text-salmon">73 percent</b> view return policies as an indication of how much a retailer cares about customers, and
            <b class="text-salmon">51 percent</b> always or often check return policies before making a purchase. Canada Post also points to more than
            <b class="text-salmon">30,000 consumer drop-off points</b> across the country, which shows how much infrastructure already exists around reverse
            logistics.
            <br /><br />
            Canada Post's Home Pickup service brings that same expectation to the doorstep. For customers, it turns returns into something that can be handled
            from home. For retailers, it may help products move back into the reverse logistics stream faster. For Canada Post, it adds a consumer-facing
            service at the exact point where e-commerce, returns, and last-mile trust overlap.
            <br /><br />
            The demand is not unique to Canada. FedEx's
            <a href="https://newsroom.fedex.com/newsroom/global-english/fedex-report-consumers-demand-greater-flexibility-in-returns" class="underline">
            2025 Returns Survey</a>, conducted with Morning Consult, found that <b class="text-salmon">20 percent</b> of Gen Z and millennials opt for home
            pickup when returning online purchases, while two-thirds of respondents consider return policies before making a purchase.
        </div>

        <!-- LIMITATIONS -->
        <h4 class="pt-6 pb-4 text-left font-sansmd text-lg font-medium leading-[1.2] tracking-tight text-brightgreen ztablet:text-lg">
            The Convenience Gap at the Doorstep
        </h4>
        <div class="pb-10 text-press text-white text-opacity-80 ztablet:text-left ztablet:text-press">
            The challenge is that home pickup does not remove the final handoff. It moves that handoff to the customer's doorstep.
            <br /><br />
            Canada Post says Home Pickup collections happen <b class="text-salmon">Monday to Friday between 9 am and 5 pm</b>, excluding weekends and statutory
            holidays. For some households, that works well. For many others, it creates a familiar tradeoff: leave the package outside and hope it remains safe,
            or stay home during a broad daytime window and wait for the pickup to happen.
            <br /><br />
            Canada Post's own guidance reflects that tradeoff. Customers in single-family homes with private entrances may leave prepared packages in a safe,
            visible spot near the door at their own risk, or hand them directly to the mail carrier. Customers in multi-unit buildings with shared entrances
            need to be home to hand the package over.
            <br /><br />
            That is still a blind handoff. A return package may contain a high-value item, personal information on a label, or goods that the retailer expects
            to receive back quickly. Leaving it exposed can create theft risk, weather risk, and uncertainty. Waiting for the carrier can turn a convenient
            service back into a scheduling burden. And for multi-unit residents, the service may be less flexible precisely because there is no secure,
            accessible place where the handoff can happen.
        </div>
